Concept Development
Two concepts shaping visitor engagement
Concept 1 — Race Track Display

To reflect our connection to the sport, we developed the pit display around the form of a race track, using it to map our journey through the challenges of STEM Racing. Each section operates as a pit stop, representing a distinct stage of development and the technical setbacks that shaped our progress. As visitors move across the track, they are able to follow the growth of Team Successors and understand how each challenge helped us strengthen our identity and move closer to our aim of inheriting the legacy and defining the future as a team across the wider competition journey.

Concept 2 — Interactive Mini-Game

To communicate the aerodynamics behind STEM Racing cars, we are developing an interactive mini-game built around the key engineering features explored throughout our journey as a team. Players drag and drop components such as car bodies and front and rear wings, with each option showing its weight, drag, and key specifications. This allows the player to assemble what they believe is the most effective configuration before racing against a selected team member in a 1v1 challenge.

The software uses predetermined values derived from our CFD simulations, developed with the support of our sponsor Turboflow, to calculate the race time of each configuration. Because the number of possible combinations is finite, each setup can be tested accurately, allowing the game to reflect the trade-offs and decision-making involved in the engineering process in a practical way.

Materials & Sustainability
Sustainable choices across materials and making
Paper-Based Honeycomb Cardboard

Image sourced from Benz Packaging: https://www.benz-packaging.com.

Sustainability has been treated as a core consideration throughout the full development of our pit display, shaping both material selection and the wider decisions made by the team. For the backboard and exhibition elements, we prioritised environmentally conscious materials, selecting paper-based honeycomb board, a lightweight engineered material produced from recycled paper waste and made up of 95% recycled content. In addition, all merchandise displayed within the exhibition will reflect more sustainable material choices through our work with Yetkey, our sustainability sponsor. Beyond the physical display itself, our commitment also extends to operating as a carbon-neutral team. Building on the initiative maintained throughout the competition, we will quantify the emissions associated with constructing the pit display, including the transportation of materials, and offset them through a structured tree-planting initiative so that the display is supported by more environmentally responsible decisions.

Our commitment to sustainability carries across the pit display, the car, and the wider competition.

Design Progression
From concepts to sketches, CAD, and backboard design

The pit display developed through a clear sequence, moving from concepts into sketches, then into CAD table studies and the digital backboard. Each stage resolved one part of the design before the final direction was established.

Phase 1 — Sketches
Initial Layout & Scale

The first sketch established the working scale for the backboard area. Using a ratio of 1cm:10cm, it turned the initial concept into a measured template that defined the available space, section proportions, and overall layout.

Comprehensive Layout Development

The second sketch developed that template into a clearer display plan. It fixed the main section placement and resolved the pit-stop structure well enough to guide the later CAD table work and backboard design.

Sketches → CAD & Digital

Once the sketch direction had been fixed, the work split into two outputs: the CAD table model and the digital backboard. The engineers developed the table in CAD, while the graphic designer developed the backboard with the same visuals as the portfolio. Both were then combined together as one pit display.

Working digitally allowed the structural and graphic systems to be refined in parallel.

Phase 2 — CAD & Digital
Initial CAD Translation

The first CAD model translated the scaled sketch into an initial three-dimensional table study. Using the 1cm:10cm ratio and the YAS in Schools reference dimensions, it tested how the basic structure would work in real space.

Developed CAD Layout

The second CAD model developed that base into a more resolved table design. It refined the structure, introduced the cardboard overlay and front LEDs, and accounted for the recycled-paper labels before the backboard graphics were combined with it.

Digital Backboard Design

The digital backboard translated the approved sketch structure into a finished graphic system. It retained the card elements, colour palette, and organisation established across the portfolio, website, and cinematic reel, while using a Bezier-curve Formula 1 track to connect the six pit-stop sections and guide the viewer across the information.

Phase 3 — Final Synthesis
Final Pit Display Design

The final pit display design brought the CAD table and backboard together into one layout that matched the YAS in Schools dimensions. This made the display clearer, visually stronger, and easier to understand as a whole.

The final design brings the CAD table and backboard system into one coherent display.
