Design Philosophy

Our design philosophy is centred on identifying the limits of the technical regulations and using aerodynamic principles to highlight the most effective areas within them. We then develop prototypes based on these evaluations, past designs, and real-world inspiration.

Aerodynamic Drag
D = ½ρv2CdA

D= Drag force; ρ= density; v= velocity of car; Cd= Drag coefficient; A= frontal area.

Wake: a region of turbulent, low-pressure, chaotic air that forms behind an object as it moves through a fluid, often causing pressure drag.

Pressure drag: the aerodynamic drag caused by the difference in air pressure between the front and the rear of a moving object.

Skin drag: the aerodynamic drag caused by friction between the air and the surface of a moving object.

Form drag: the aerodynamic drag created by the shape of an object and how that shape forces air to separate as it flows around it.

Stagnation pressure: the pressure at the point where the airflow hits the surface head-on and cannot move forward.

Vortices: swirling, spiral-shaped masses of air that form when high-pressure and low-pressure air mix together.

Bernoulli's Principle

Bernoulli's principle states that, as the speed of a fluid increases, its pressure decreases, causing faster-moving air to exert less pressure than slower-moving air. This is often used to create airfoils that generate a pressure differential between the top and bottom surfaces, producing lift or downforce.

Diagram showing how an airfoil may generate downforce.

Reynolds Number

Reynolds number is a unitless measure of how fast and how large an object is moving through a fluid compared with how viscous that fluid is.

Re = ρvL / μ

Re= Reynolds number; ρ= density of air; v= velocity of car; L= length of car; μ= viscosity of air.

Due to the car's small length (0.17m-0.21m), Reynolds number is low, so flow separates earlier, wakes become larger, and pressure drag becomes dominant, accounting for around 80-90% of total drag.

Rear Wing

The role of the rear wing in a typical Formula 1 car is to generate downforce, increasing speed through corners. In our case, where we race in a straight line, downforce serves little purpose and may instead create drag. This means the rear wing on our car should aim to minimise both drag and lift/downforce. Through our analysis, we found that symmetrical airfoils such as NACA 0009, 0010, and 0012, at an angle of attack near 0, produced the best results.

3D-printed front and rear wing design.

Before 3D printing, measured tolerance was added to account for sanding and ensure accuracy. The CAD file was then processed in UltiMaker Cura using high refinement, adaptive layers, and a low layer height to produce smoother curved surfaces before printing. The printed model was then sanded progressively from 200-grit to 1000-grit, following the direction of airflow to minimise turbulence-causing micro-grooves.

Software
Autodesk Fusion 360

Autodesk Fusion 360 is our primary 3D design software, as it integrates CAD and CAM while also allowing us to calculate the mass of each prototype.

ANSYS Discovery

Although ANSYS Discovery does not provide highly accurate results, it offers an intuitive flow trace that helps us quickly visualise areas for improvement.

Turboflow

Turboflow is a cloud-based OpenFOAM solver that provides a race-time calculator, competitive results, and direct use from Fusion 360.
